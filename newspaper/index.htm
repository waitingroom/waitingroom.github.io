Between places with
Republica Austral

A conversation about the journey of objects.

Q: The book "Taxing Art" by Beta Tank explains how the Value Added Tax (VAT) is applied to an object in two ways in Germany. The object categorized as functional is more expensive than the object considered art or non-functional. In your case (Switzerland and specially República Austral), how does the categorization of objects work? How do they see affected by this?

A: In Switzerland it is the same: Artworks are exempt from value added tax (VAT) if the artwork is worked by the artist himself and produced by his own hand. The artwork must be free of purpose, i. e. exclusively intended or suitable for contemplation (images, paintings, sculptures, statues and decorative works of art). As soon as the artworks are produced in series, they are considered as craft products and therefore VAT is applied. The case of Republica Austral is different for several reasons: Our objects are created by our designers and not by ourselves, even if we can give impulses or guidelines. They are produced in small series, some are functional, others destined to art galleries. Therefore, the design objects of Republica Austral are subject to VAT, which here in Switzerland is at 8%.
Q: After the creation of República Austral, what changed in your perception regarding international policies about design, having Switzerland and South America as examples?

A: There are several factors that directly influence us, we would like to highlight two. Free trade agreements first. We work with Argentina, Chile and Uruguay and the political and economic situation of each country is very different. For example, with Chile Switzerland signed a free trade agreement in 2004 and trade relations in general are very good. This affects Republica Austral in the sense that our objects are totally or partially exempt from customs duties. On the other hand, Argentina and Uruguay are part of MERCOSUR and only in January 2017 a declaration of intent has been signed to retake negotiations for a free trade agreement between MERCOSUR and EFTA (European Free Trade Association that includes Switzerland, Norway, Iceland and Liechtenstein). It can take years until an agreement is reached. Especially in the case of Argentina, today we are confronted with an incomparable bureaucracy that makes the export and cash flow processes considerably more difficult. In all cases, it is essential for us that designers collaborate closely with us and help us, for example, to find the best shipping solution and improve export processes. Secondly, we would mention currency fluctuations. In general, developing countries that are heavily dependent on the export of primary resources such as copper, gas or oil are showing greater currency fluctuations. In the case of Argentina, a significant inflation is added to the Argentine peso. This inevitably affects us and the time factor – the right moment to make an investment - is very important and at the same time difficult to anticipate.
Q: In which way you think government policies affect creativity and innovation?

A: There are several factors. In Switzerland, for example, we have a dual education system, i. e. academic education is complemented by a direct and practice-oriented vocational education. During an apprenticeship of 3 to 4 years young people are trained directly in companies, completed for 1 to 2 days per week of school classes. The vast majority of young people take this path and only minorities follow an academic education. This educational system is a fundamental factor for Switzerland's innovative capacity and economic efficiency because there is a close relationship between business and training. Moreover, Switzerland is a country that does not have any primary resources such as oil, gas or metals. Historically the country was forced to take other paths and since the 19th century it began to create industries based on technological progress. Initially it was mainly the textile and watchmaking industries, then the industries of precision machinery and the service sector such as tourism, banks etc. began to develop. Innovation in Switzerland is almost 100 percent linked to technological progress, which is also reflected in the creations of local designers. As mentioned above, in South America creativity and innovation come out of a resource constraint. Creators are forced to investigate new production processes and generally develop them together with their artisans because they depend on their capacity. Then there are also more practical ways to encourage creativity and innovation. Governments can award scholarships to study abroad or to present design objects at leading fairs in Europe. Chile has promoted it a lot in previous years, Uruguay currently has very active institutions that promote Uruguayan design inside and outside the country. On the other hand, in Argentina we don't observe many activities in this sense and designers depend on themselves.
Republica Austral 2017. Claudio Riquelme and Regula Brand. Photographer: Felipe Fontecilla


Q: How do the distance and the mailing international policies affect the designers who collaborate with you?

A: Transport is undoubtedly one of our biggest challenges. The distances are very long and the economies are relatively small, resulting in very high transport costs. This has a direct replica in sales prices. At the same time, in order to be able to compete in the European market it is necessary that we position ourselves with a good quality/price ratio, at the cost of a lower profit margin. This also has implications for our designers. They have to constantly seek to improve their production processes in order to produce more efficiently or to find ways to reduce the weight and volume of shipments without impairing functionality and especially the quality of objects.

The supernatural and the everyday
of living in another country

Series of excerpts from informal conversations about the Effect
of immigrating on Artistic Production.

Sue Hellen is a graphic project from Santiago, Chile. Currently in Valencia, Spain

Q: Living in Valencia, what role do you think art or design plays in geopolitics today?

A: Our globalized society is a virtual territory that is very powerful and has many possibilities to approach many aspects. I'm not sure that the real art circuit works like international biennials where each pavilion seeks to stop declaring or creating an image of a country. Most artists respond to codes that do not represent a specific culture or territory, but aesthetic sensibilities. It is not my intention to ignore political art, specific place or art that speaks of the situations of countries or cultures that of course exists, is very powerful and has a presence. I must add that I feel that art develops hegemonically in certain cultural capitals -euroamerican, and others in the East-, which are not expanding, nor are they developing in different territories, which means a migration of creators towards them, fostering the concentration, elitization and segregation of art, making its actuality only accessible to those who have the means to access these, the artistic circuit being inserted in the dynamics of capital domination.
Q: I have seen Bibendum repeatedly in your work, which is what led me to ask you these questions, what is the implication of using this character?

A: It turns out to be a very powerful character at a communicational level, it is inserted in the popular imagination of the whole globe. That image is very attractive since it is a perfect character to decontextualize, ironize and pervert.
Sue Hellen and Mogwai.

THE OTHER HALF

—

Diego is the other half of Funken art studio based in Santiago, Chile. Now he is making a living in New York, Brooklyn.

Q: How has the socio-political situation of the United States affected your work or its methodology?

A: The trump situation in power and the problems related to it do not feel much in New York at least on a day-to-day basis in terms of social experiences, interactions with people, etc. This city is mostly anti trump and is super cosmopolitan. This means that the city has adapted and maintains a certain respect for multi-culture, for differences in gender, color, etc. Without a doubt it is inevitable not to think about the situation when developing a project. The focus changes. Now everything is thought from a more "activist" perspective or at least try to approach it like that. More guerrilla, more protest, more attempt to generate some awareness.
Diego Salinas
Funken Studio, NY.
Photographer: Nicolas Salinas


CONTEXT

—

Olga is a graphic designer + art director from Mexico living and working in Basel, CH.

Q: Do you think that living in Basel influences your discipline and work?

A: Sure! Having a new context influences my work, coming to a different city, meeting people and being surrounded by new references of design are shaping a new stage of my practice. Besides, I have always had the Swiss design as a reference and being here has helped me to understand it better.

Q: What were the reasons to leave Mexico?

A: Without any particular reasons but I always wanted to try something different after university and before graduating there was the opportunity to come to Basel, a city I had never heard of, so I said why not?

Q: From the contrast in the quality of life in Basel with respect to Mexico, what do you think about the socio-political realities of each country and its influence on art and design?

A: Mexico and Switzerland are completely different worlds, each with pros and cons. Socioeconomically speaking Switzerland is better, the economy is stronger what makes it a good country to be an artist or designer.
